# Project milestone — an honest data study

Everything in Part I converges on one deliverable: **an honest, well-documented data study on a dataset you sourced and enriched yourself, communicated visually to a non-technical audience.**

## What to produce

Concretely, that means you:

- **Find or collect** a dataset you genuinely care about — and enrich it with at least one column you gathered yourself ([Chapters 2](chapter-2.md) and [9](chapter-9.md)).
- **Get it into a dataframe** and make it trustworthy — cleaned deliberately, with variable types and categories sorted out, and combined with any extra tables you need ([Chapters 1](chapter-1.md), [3](chapter-3.md), [4](chapter-4.md) and [5](chapter-5.md)).
- **Explore** it until you understand its real shape rather than the shape you hoped for ([Chapter 6](chapter-6.md)).
- **Build a clear visual argument**, and ideally a small interactive view that lets others explore it ([Chapters 7](chapter-7.md) and [8](chapter-8.md)).
- **Keep a provenance note** throughout, so a reader can see exactly where your numbers came from and what you did to them ([Chapter 4](chapter-4.md)).

## How it's assessed

The assessment is not "did you find something impressive." It is closer to a **design defence**: can you *walk through* your study and account for your choices — why this dataset, what it can and can't say, what you cleaned and why, where you might be wrong?

!!! practice "A rough rubric to aim at"
    - **Sourcing & provenance** — is the origin documented, and is at least one column genuinely collected/enriched by you?
    - **Trustworthiness** — are cleaning decisions deliberate and recorded, not silent?
    - **Analysis** — does the exploration show real understanding of the data's shape (skew, outliers, missingness), not just a headline average?
    - **Communication** — does the visual argument suit the decision and the audience, with an honest axis and no chartjunk?
    - **Honesty** — can you name the study's limits and one thing that might be wrong?

That last row is the one that separates treating data as a design material from merely producing charts: the goal is not the most striking graphic, but work you can **stand behind and explain**.

## Looking ahead

Part II raises the stakes, because there the "dataset" becomes a system that *learns* — and reading those honestly is harder still. Everything you built here (sourcing, cleaning, exploring, visualising, and above all documenting your choices) is the foundation the next part stands on.
